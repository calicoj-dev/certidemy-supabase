# Certification Scheme — Scrum Developer I — AI (SD-AI-I)

**Scheme owner:** Certidemy (the certification body)
**Scheme code:** SD-AI-I
**Document version:** 1.0
**Status:** Active scheme of record
**Last updated:** July 2026

---

## About this document

This is the **certification scheme** for the Scrum Developer I — AI credential:
the published contract describing what the credential certifies, how a candidate
earns it, and how Certidemy keeps that decision fair, consistent, and defensible.
It is written to align with the structure of the ISO/IEC 17024 framework for
bodies operating certification of persons, and mirrors the SM-AI-I and SPO-AI-I
schemes so the three Level-I credentials read as one coherent system.

**A note on accreditation status.** Certidemy is **not currently an accredited
certification body**. This scheme is designed to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework —
"audit-ready by design" — so that the credential is structurally legitimate now
and the body can pursue formal accreditation once operational history and
candidate data exist. Where a requirement depends on data or governance that only
accumulates after launch, this document says so plainly rather than overstating
readiness.

## 2. Purpose and scope

The SD-AI-I credential certifies that the holder understands the **Developers'**
accountability within the Scrum framework as defined by the 2020 Scrum Guide
**and** can apply the engineering craft of building and verifying the Increment —
quality, the Definition of Done, testing, integration — in a team where AI tooling
is part of how work gets done.

The distinguishing scope of this credential is that AI fluency is **woven through
every domain**, not isolated in a bolt-on "AI domain." The signature competency is
**AI-Augmented Implementation**: implementing an acceptance-criteria-bearing
specification with an AI coding assistant as a teammate, while the Developer
remains accountable for verifying that output against the Definition of Done. The
scheme tests, across its domains, the Developer's enduring accountabilities in an
AI-augmented setting. The examination tests, across its domains, that
AI-generated code is *unverified work* until inspected;
that the Definition of Done applies identically whether a human or an AI produced
the code; that generation is cheap and verification is expensive, which makes the
discipline of "Done" matter more, not less; and that accountability for the
Increment is human and collective and cannot be delegated to a tool.

This credential is the Developer's half of **Spec-Driven Development**: where the
Product Owner (SPO-AI-I) authors a Product Backlog item precise enough to act as an
agent-executable specification, the Scrum Developer implements that specification
with AI and owns its verification. Together with SM-AI-I, the three Level-I
credentials certify the whole AI-era Scrum Team, role by role.

**Target audience.** Practicing or aspiring software developers, testers, and other
Increment-builders working in Scrum, in environments where AI coding tools are in
active use.

**Out of scope.** This credential does not certify product-ownership or
value-decision authority (SPO-AI-I), Scrum Master / process facilitation (SM-AI-I),
proficiency in any specific programming language or AI tool, or AI/ML system
development itself.

## 3. Eligibility

The SD-AI-I examination operates under **open eligibility**: there are no mandatory
prerequisites, formal training hours, or prior-credential requirements to attempt
the examination. This is an entry-tier ("I") credential, and the body has chosen
open access as a deliberate scheme policy.

Candidates are strongly advised to complete the associated body-of-knowledge
coursework (or to possess equivalent practical experience) before attempting the
secure examination, but completion of Certidemy coursework is **not** a condition
of eligibility and confers no advantage in the secure examination (§8).

---

## 4. Body of knowledge

The body of knowledge is organized into **5 domains** comprising **44 assessable
tasks**, supported by **135 defined concepts** and delivered through **5
instructional modules / 44 lessons** (authored across the credential languages;
see the open content note in §11).

The five domains and their examination weightings:

| Domain | Title | Weight | Tasks |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Agile Foundations & Empirical Engineering in the AI Era | 12.5% | 6 |
| D2 | The Scrum Framework from the Developers' Seat | 15.0% | 8 |
| D3 | Engineering Craft, Quality & the Definition of Done | 20.0% | 10 |
| D4 | AI-Augmented Implementation (signature) | 30.0% | 12 |
| D5 | Collaboration, Professionalism & Continuous Improvement | 22.5% | 8 |

All **44 tasks are within examination scope.** AI-specific competencies are
distributed across all five domains, with the heaviest weighting (D4, 30%) on
AI-Augmented Implementation — the scheme's signature area, and the Developer's half
of Spec-Driven Development.

The authoritative reference for Scrum content is the **2020 Scrum Guide**.
Engineering-craft and AI-era tasks are grounded in the concept definitions of the
body of knowledge and in established software-engineering and AI-assisted-development
practice; they do not depend on any third-party vendor's product or any specific AI
tool.

## 5. Competency model and job-task basis

The domain-and-task structure is a **job-task model**: each of the 44 tasks
describes what a competent Developer must know or do, and each task is linked to the
concepts it draws on and to the assessment items that test it. This produces a
complete, queryable **traceability matrix** from domain → task → concept →
assessment item → instructional lesson (§10).

**Validation status — stated honestly.** The competency model was derived from the
2020 Scrum Guide, established software-engineering practice, and analysis of the
competencies introduced by AI-augmented software development. **Independent
subject-matter-expert (SME) panel validation of the job-task analysis is planned
but not yet completed**, and is a prerequisite for accreditation.

## 6. Examination structure

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Number of items | 80 |
| Duration | 90 minutes |
| Item format | Single-best-answer multiple choice and true/false |
| Delivery language | Candidate-selected: en, es-419, or pt-BR |
| Scoring | Dichotomous (correct / incorrect); no negative marking |

**Blueprint (item allocation by domain).** Each form draws items proportionally to
the domain weightings, yielding this target distribution at 80 items:

| Domain | Weight | Items per form (target) |
|---|---|---|
| D1 | 12.5% | 10 |
| D2 | 15.0% | 12 |
| D3 | 20.0% | 16 |
| D4 | 30.0% | 24 |
| D5 | 22.5% | 18 |

The examination assembler draws each form from the secure item bank according to
this blueprint and **refuses to issue a form if any domain is short of its quota**,
guaranteeing every candidate receives a blueprint-valid form.

**Cognitive level.** Secure multiple-choice items are authored across Bloom levels
1–4 (Remember through Analyze), weighted toward Apply and Analyze so the
examination tests applied judgment, not recall alone. Higher-order performance
items (Bloom 5–6) are reserved for future AI-driven simulation assessments and are
not part of the current secure multiple-choice examination.

## 7. Pass mark and standard-setting

**Current pass mark:** 80% (64 of 80 items).

**Methodology — stated honestly.** The pass mark is **criterion-referenced**: it
reflects the level of mastery the body judges necessary to certify competence, not
a curve relative to other candidates. The current 80% threshold is a **provisional,
expert-judgment cut score** set at the "I"-tier standard, consistent across the
Level-I suite.

It has **not yet** been validated by a formal standard-setting study, because
defensible standard-setting (e.g., a modified-Angoff panel and statistical
confirmation against candidate performance) **requires real candidate data that
does not yet exist.** The body's plan is to collect candidate response data through
live operation, compute item-level statistics and form reliability, then convene a
standard-setting panel to confirm or adjust the cut score against that evidence.
Until then, the 80% pass mark is held as the documented, criterion-referenced
standard and applied uniformly to every candidate and form. Until a formal
standard-setting study is completed, the 80% threshold serves as the documented,
expert-judgment-based passing standard applied uniformly to all candidates.

## 8. Assessment integrity and separation of functions

**Separation of training and certification.** Certidemy operates a **technical
separation** between the learning function and the certification function through a
strict item-pool firewall:

- The **practice pool** (used by the adaptive learning engine and study tools) and
  the **secure pool** (used only to assemble the live certification examination) are
  entirely distinct sets of items.
- Secure items are **never linked into the practice/learning graph**, so no secure
  examination content is reachable through study, review, or practice. The secure
  examination is **inert to the adaptive learning engine.**
- Completion of coursework confers **no advantage** on the secure examination and is
  not a condition of eligibility (§3).

**Governance-level separation** (an impartiality structure ensuring certification
decisions are free from commercial or training-side influence) is a formal
accreditation requirement on the body's roadmap; it is named here as
established-in-design, to-be-formalized-operationally.

**Item bank inventory.** The item bank target holds, **per assessable task**, 24
secure items and 30 practice items — i.e. 8 secure + 10 practice **per language**
across en / es-419 / pt-BR, for all 44 tasks. Aggregate: **1,056 secure** and
**1,320 practice** items.

| Pool | Per task (all langs) | Per language per task | Bank total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure (examination) | 24 | 8 | 1,056 |
| Practice (learning) | 30 | 10 | 1,320 |

**Form variation.** The secure bank provides substantial over-coverage of every
domain's blueprint quota — every domain sits at roughly 3–5× its per-form quota in
each language — so forms vary candidate-to-candidate and no single form approaches
exposure of the bank.

**Credential verification (public).** Every issued credential carries a unique
credential code and a public verification record. Any third party can validate a
credential's authenticity, holder name, issue date, and current validity status
from its verification code at Certidemy's public verification endpoint, with no
account required.

## 9. Recertification

SD-AI-I credentials are valid for **1 year** from issuance.

**Rationale.** The credential certifies competence in software development *as
practiced in AI-augmented teams* — a domain whose tools and practices are changing
rapidly. A 1-year cycle keeps a current credential a genuine signal of current
competence. Recertification re-tests against the then-current body of knowledge.

---

## 10. Traceability and coverage

The scheme maintains a complete, queryable traceability matrix:

**domain → task → concept → assessment item**, and
**concept → instructional lesson**

Two audit-relevant guarantees follow:

1. **Coverage** — every one of the 44 in-scope tasks is supported by assessment
   items in both pools, in all three languages, and is taught by at least one lesson
   per language.
2. **No untaught testing** — every concept assessed on the examination is present in
   the published body of knowledge, and every examination item resolves back to the
   lesson(s) that teach its concept(s).

The projection of lessons into this matrix is maintained by an automated,
re-runnable control rather than by hand, so the matrix stays complete as content
evolves. Task-to-concept reachability is established at seed time, so no concept is
orphaned from the tasks that reference it.

## 11. Scheme maintenance and governance

**Review cadence.** Reviewed when the underlying Scrum Guide is revised, when the
job-task analysis is re-validated, or at minimum annually.

**Change control.** Changes to domains, weightings, the item bank, or the pass mark
are versioned and recorded, and the blueprint a credential was earned against is
snapshotted at issuance so a credential always reflects the standard in force when
it was awarded.

**Open content note.** At the time of writing, SD-AI-I is in content production: the
certification skeleton (domains, tasks, concepts, and their traceability) is
established, and the instructional lessons and both item pools are being authored
toward the per-task targets in §8. The credential is **not published for candidates
until every domain clears its blueprint quota in all three languages** and the
assembler reports a valid form. This is stated transparently rather than implied as
complete.

**Open items on the path to accreditation** (named transparently):

- Independent SME-panel validation of the job-task analysis and weightings (§5).
- Formal standard-setting study and psychometric validation of the cut score, using
  candidate data collected in live operation (§7).
- Governance-level impartiality structure separating the certification decision from
  commercial and training functions (§8).
- Operational records required by the standard: appeals, complaints handling,
  examination-integrity / identity procedures, and internal audit.
- Completion of instructional lessons and both item pools across en / es-419 / pt-BR
  (§11, above).

The credential is positioned as **"built to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework /
audit-ready by design,"** not as accredited, until accreditation is formally
achieved.
