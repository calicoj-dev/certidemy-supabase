# AISM-I — Job-Task Analysis (JTA)

**Credential:** Certidemy AI Service Management I — AI
**Code:** AISM-I  ·  **Tier:** I (single-correct-answer)  ·  **Cert UUID:** *(assigned at scaffold, Stage 6)*
**Program / category:** Certidemy AI Governance & Service Management (category slug
`governance-service-management`, sort_order 4). AISM-I is filed under this existing program
alongside AIGRM-I; it does not found a new category. Rationale in the program note below.
**Status:** **Locked scheme-of-record** — pre-launch and pre-scaffold. External review (Grok)
complete and recommended lock (8.8/10); SME-panel validation still pending (an accreditation-path
item). This is the single source of truth: **no version lineage is kept pre-launch.** Formal
versioning begins at launch, when live edits must be tracked against real candidate data.
**Basis:** Stage-1 BoK on modern AI-era service management. Built on the *facts* of
contemporary service management (service value, value co-creation, the lifecycle, the
standard management practices, AIOps) as field-standard, non-ownable knowledge — the same
posture used with the 2020 Scrum Guide. AISM-I is authored in our own words and its own
structure; it is **not** presented as, and does not imply it is, ITIL or any third-party
program (marketing-identity line, below).

> **Fixed facts.**
> - **6 domains**, weights summing to 100 (D1 12.5% · D2 15% · D3 17.5% · D4 27.5% signature ·
>   D5 15% · D6 12.5%).
> - **61 tasks** · **~224 concept rows** (see per-domain totals) · **80 items/form**.
> - **MCQ ceiling `4_analyze`;** all 61 tasks are exam-scope (none Bloom 5/6).
> - **Computed cognitive profile (a result, not a target):** 0% Remember · **58.4% Understand**
>   · **32.8% Apply** · **8.8% Analyze**. `exam_blueprint` is set from this at scaffold and
>   machine-verified by invariant 17 — never hand-edited ahead of the tasks.
> - **Exam (proposed, reconcile at Stage 12):** 80 items · 80% pass · single-best-answer ·
>   ~120 min (80 × 90 s/item per the engine addendum). AISM-II (best-of-four situational
>   judgment) reuses this spine but needs the L2 generator — not this cert.

> **Program-placement note.** AISM-I is filed under the **Certidemy AI Governance & Service
> Management** program (slug `governance-service-management`), shared with AIGRM-I. It does not
> found its own category.
> - **Why combined, not separate.** A market-convergence search found buyers and the market
>   trending toward **integrated AI-governance-and-service-management functions** — the same
>   organizations increasingly own *both* "govern AI responsibly" and "run AI-augmented services"
>   under one remit. The convergence is directional, not yet complete, but a catalog should track
>   where buyers are going, and this program does.
> - **D5 is the seam that justifies the grouping.** Domain D5 ("Governing AI in Service
>   Management") is precisely where governance meets operations — the AIGRM-I-bridge concepts are
>   the literal connective tissue between the two.
> - **The role test holds across both clusters.** The program holds governance roles (AIGRM
>   risk/governance officer · AI Assurance & Audit · Responsible AI & Ethics) **and**
>   service-management roles (AISM service manager · AI Service Operations / SRE · Cloud Service
>   Management) — a populated, coherent program, not a thin one.

> **Design intent (distributed AI transformation, honestly bounded).** AISM-I's
> differentiation is **structural, not decorative**: the AI transformation is taught *at each
> foundation that genuinely bends under AI*, so the AI-native character is visible throughout
> D1–D3 — not quarantined into a few "AI-thread" tasks, and not sprayed as flavor onto every
> lesson. Two failure modes are avoided deliberately:
> - **The bolt-on** (the field-standard entry syllabus): AI as a single low-single-digit slice
>   in an appendix. This under-sells a credential meant to be AI-native.
> - **The AI-wash**: an AI paragraph tacked onto every task whether or not the concept changes.
>   Under expert scrutiny this reads as marketing, and a hostile reviewer torches it faster than
>   honest foundations. It is also untestable — decorative AI ties to no assessable concept.
>
> **The honesty firewall (the rule that governs every AI concept here).** A foundation task gains
> an AI dimension **only where a specific, testable "what does a practitioner do differently
> because of AI?" exists.** Warranty genuinely changes for non-deterministic output; outputs-vs-
> outcomes genuinely worsens when AI mass-produces outputs; a model update is genuinely a change;
> SLAs genuinely must commit to bounds not sameness — so AI lives *inside* those foundations.
> Where a foundation does **not** change under AI — the definition of the sponsor/customer/user
> roles, governance vs management, the value-system components — the task stays **clean**. Keeping
> those honest is not a gap; it is what makes the woven AI credible rather than AI-washed.
>
> **Result.** A genuine AI dimension is present in **43 of 61 tasks (~70%)**; the fully AI-native
> operations-and-governance core (**D4 + D5 = 42.5%** of exam weight) remains the heaviest, most
> applied part of the cert; and **18 tasks stay honestly AI-free** because their concept does not
> transform. The differentiation is teachable, testable, and defensible under fire — every AI
> concept is load-bearing, none is paint. A holder who cannot define a service, an SLA, or an
> incident is not innovative but incomplete, so the foundations are taught straight; AI is added
> where it is real, and only there.
>
> **Completeness posture (the buyer-comparison test).** AISM-I is built to survive a side-by-side
> syllabus comparison with the current field-standard AI-era ITSM frameworks. The subjects those
> frameworks now treat as essential — AI-native operations, unified product-and-service management,
> **experience management**, AI governance, and **sustainability** — are field knowledge no one
> owns; AISM-I covers them all, in its own words and structure, and goes *further* on the AI
> dimension. Two competencies were added specifically to close visible gaps a comparing buyer would
> otherwise exploit: **experience, trust, and sustainable service (D6)** and **supplier /
> AI-model-dependency management (3.12)**. What AISM-I does **not** do is reproduce any framework's
> proprietary constructs, branded terms, or claim of equivalence (marketing-identity line). We cover
> and extend the *knowledge*; we do not clone the *framework*. A buyer scanning this JTA should find
> no essential modern competency missing — and several the general frameworks treat generically that
> AISM-I owns with an AI-native depth (trust in AI services, the compute/energy footprint of AI, the
> dependency risk of models you do not own).

> **Marketing-identity line (sacred gate #6).** Field-standard framework names may
> appear in **lesson bodies** as pedagogical cross-reference ("this practice is often
> called X"). They are **forbidden in marketing and app chrome** wherever the copy
> would claim or imply Certidemy *is* that program, presents its syllabus as ours, or
> makes a comparative "beyond framework X" identity claim. Internal identifiers stay
> untouched. Standard nouns (service, value co-creation, lifecycle, incident, change)
> are the field's shared vocabulary and are used plainly — inventing novel names for
> standard concepts to "look original" only makes the credential illegible to employers.

> **Design notes (durable decisions behind this JTA).**
> - The competency model was derived from the field-standard body of service-management knowledge
>   and current AI-operations practice, structured on the AIGRM-I model and the cognitive model,
>   and refined across external-review iterations (Grok). SME-panel validation is still pending.
> - Every profile figure is **computed from task decisions made on merits**; the profile is never
>   chosen and the tasks bent to fit it. Commercial "differentiation" pressure is routed to
>   positioning/marketing (scheme §2, program page), never allowed to distort task statements.
> - **AI integration follows the honesty firewall** (design-intent note above): AI concepts sit
>   with the foundation tasks that genuinely transform under AI, and foundations that do not
>   transform stay clean. This distributes the AI-native character across D1–D3 without
>   decorative AI, and it is the deliberate answer to "make the AI transformation visible
>   throughout" — visible where it is real, absent where it is not.
> - D3 task 3.6 (root-cause analysis, `4_analyze`) is the one diagnostic-reasoning task outside
>   the AI operations domain, included on validity grounds — real service operations require it.

## Field legend

The JTA is the traceability spine: **domain → task → concept**. It seeds the scaffold
(domains, tasks, `task_concepts`, concepts); lessons tag these codes/slugs; questions
ground in the concepts; coverage proves against them.

- **Task code** — stable `D.N` id, **immutable once published**.
- **Task statement** — what a competent practitioner can do; verb-first.
- **Bloom** — primary cognitive level on the DB enum: `2_understand`, `3_apply`,
  `4_analyze`. Assigned from the task's verb + KSAs, one per task. Foundations skew
  understand; applied domains rise to apply/analyze. (Rule: item Bloom == task Bloom.)
- **Freq / Crit** — job-task frequency × criticality (`H`/`M`/`L`); justifies domain
  weighting and per-task item emphasis.
- **Concepts** — one teachable/testable idea each (kebab-case slug). A concept shared
  by ≥2 AISM-I tasks is marked *(D#)* (a real within-cert `task_concepts` reuse link,
  defined once). A concept that is the **same idea as an AIGRM-I concept** is marked
  *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — mechanically a new AISM-I concept row (concepts are cert-scoped),
  but authored and taught consistently with its AIGRM-I sibling so the two certs read as
  one governance spine. KSA triple (knowledge/skills/abilities) is derived per task at
  scaffold from the statement + concepts, per CERT-SCHEMA-GUIDE.

# D1 — Foundations of Digital Product & Service Management  *(12.5%)*

**Intent.** The shared vocabulary and mental model the credential builds on — service,
value co-creation, the parties in a service relationship, utility/warranty/experience,
outcomes, SLAs. Predominantly `2_understand`; makes the applied domains legible. **AI is
woven through most of D1 at the points where a foundation genuinely changes**: what an AI
service *is* (1.2), co-creation under uncertainty (1.3), warranty for non-deterministic
output (1.5), the output-volume trap (1.6), SLAs for AI services (1.7), and the provider's
continuing accountability for AI behavior (1.8). One task stays deliberately clean — the
sponsor/customer/user roles (1.4) do not change under AI, and pretending otherwise would
be AI-wash.

### D1 concepts
- `service-management-purpose` — coordinating people, process, and technology to deliver value reliably through services.
- `service-management-value` — why a disciplined approach beats ad-hoc delivery: consistency, reliability, accountability.
- `it-to-digital-shift` — the field's move from managing IT services to managing digital products and services end-to-end.
- `ai-augmented-service-shift` — the current wave of that same evolution: services with AI embedded in how they are run; the purpose (reliable value) is unchanged, the material is new.
- `service-definition` — a means of enabling value co-creation by helping customers get outcomes without owning specific costs and risks.
- `product-definition` — a configuration of resources, created by the organization, that will deliver value to consumers.
- `digital-product` — a product delivered and consumed digitally, evolving continuously rather than shipped once.
- `digital-service` — a service enabled by or delivered through digital technology, often software-defined.
- `product-vs-service` — a product is what is built and configured; a service is how its value is made available and consumed.
- `value-co-creation` — value emerges from the active collaboration of provider and consumer, not from one-way delivery.
- `value-not-delivered` — the provider cannot hand over value alone; the consumer's use and context complete it.
- `provider-contribution` — what the provider brings: resources, capability, the service offering.
- `consumer-contribution` — what the consumer brings: their goals, context, and use that realize the outcome.
- `service-relationship` — the cooperation between a provider and a consumer across a service's life.
- `provider-consumer` — the two primary parties; one supplies the service, one uses it.
- `sponsor-customer-user` — the consumer-side roles: who authorizes budget, who defines requirements, who uses the service.
- `service-relationship-types` — the forms a relationship takes (e.g., basic provision, cooperation, partnership).
- `utility` — fitness for purpose: what the service does (functionality).
- `warranty` — fitness for use: assurance the service performs (availability, capacity, security, continuity).
- `user-experience` — the whole of a user's interaction with, and perceptions of, a service.
- `fit-for-purpose-fit-for-use` — a service delivers value only when it is both useful and dependable.
- `output-vs-outcome` — an output is a deliverable; an outcome is the result the consumer actually needs.
- `cost-and-risk` — value has two sides: costs/risks a service removes for the consumer, and costs/risks it imposes.
- `value-equation` — value weighed as outcomes and experience against the costs and risks incurred.
- `service-quality` — the degree to which a service meets expectations across utility, warranty, and experience.
- `service-level` — a defined, measured target for an aspect of a service.
- `sla` — a documented agreement of expected service levels between provider and consumer.
- `sla-measurement-pitfall` — reporting every level "green" while the consumer is dissatisfied; measure the outcome, not just the metric.
- `ai-infused-service` *(taught at 1.2)* — a service whose behavior is partly produced by an AI model, changing how it is defined and assured.
- `co-creation-with-ai-service` *(taught at 1.3)* — when the provider's contribution is a probabilistic model, the consumer co-creates value under uncertainty; assurance and expectation-setting change accordingly.
- `non-deterministic-output` *(taught at 1.5)* — an AI service may give different valid outputs for the same input; warranty shifts from promising a fixed result to assuring bounds — quality range, safety, and escalation.
- `ai-output-volume-trap` *(taught at 1.6)* — AI can generate outputs (drafts, replies, classifications) at high volume, making activity look like achievement and sharpening the risk of counting outputs as outcomes.
- `ai-service-level` *(taught at 1.7)* — a service level for a non-deterministic service defines acceptable quality ranges, safety limits, and human-escalation paths rather than a single guaranteed output.
- `ai-as-service-action` *(taught at 1.8)* — an AI-generated response is itself a service action for which the provider remains responsible; "the model did it" is not a defense.
- `service-that-drifts` *(taught at 1.8)* — an AI service's behavior can degrade over time as data and conditions shift, even with no code change.
- `provider-owns-ai-behavior` *(taught at 1.8)* — the provider is answerable for what its AI service does and for detecting when it drifts; stability must be monitored, not assumed.

*D1: 8 tasks · 35 concepts · Bloom 8×understand. AI dimension in 7 of 8 tasks (1.4 clean).*

# D2 — The Value System, Guiding Principles & Governance  *(15%)*

**Intent.** The organizing system that turns opportunity and demand into value: the
value-system components, the seven guiding principles (understood *and* applied), the
continual-improvement model, and governance as direction-and-control. **D2 is deliberately
the most stable domain** — the value system, the principles' purpose, and the
governance/management distinction do not themselves change under AI, so those tasks are
taught clean (forcing AI onto them would be exactly the AI-wash this JTA avoids). AI enters
where it genuinely bears: judging AI-adoption decisions with the principles (2.4), applying
"optimize and automate" to AI (2.5), and why AI services make continual improvement
structural rather than optional (2.8). The applied tasks (2.4, 2.5, 2.9) assess the
competence, not the scenario dressing.

### D2 concepts
- `service-value-system` — the model of how all the organization's components and activities work together to create value.
- `svs-components` — the parts of the value system (principles, governance, the value chain, practices, continual improvement).
- `svs-inputs-outputs` — opportunity and demand enter the system; value results.
- `guiding-principles` — universal recommendations that guide an organization in all circumstances.
- `universal-guidance` — the principles apply regardless of the framework, tool, or change being pursued.
- `principles-interact` — the principles reinforce one another and are used together, not in isolation.
- `focus-on-value` — everything done should link, directly or indirectly, to value for stakeholders.
- `start-where-you-are` — assess and build on what already exists rather than starting over.
- `progress-iteratively-with-feedback` — work in small increments, using feedback to adjust.
- `collaborate-and-promote-visibility` — work across boundaries and make work and information visible.
- `think-and-work-holistically` — no component works in isolation; consider the whole.
- `keep-it-simple-and-practical` — use the minimum steps to accomplish the objective; eliminate what adds no value.
- `optimize-and-automate` — optimize a process first, then automate what remains where it adds value.
- `principle-application` — using a principle to reason toward a sound decision in a real situation.
- `principle-selection` — recognizing which principle a situation most calls for.
- `principle-tradeoffs` — principles can pull in different directions; applying them means weighing them.
- `ai-adoption-judgment` — using the principles (focus on value, keep it simple, start where you are) to decide whether AI genuinely serves the outcome or is being adopted for its own sake.
- `optimize-before-automate` — automating an inefficient or broken process only scales the problem.
- `automation-candidacy` — judging whether a task is a good candidate for automation at all.
- `do-not-automate-a-broken-process` — fix or retire a flawed process before automating it.
- `ai-automation-judgment` — deciding where AI-driven automation genuinely adds value versus where it adds risk.
- `ai-changes-automation-calculus` — AI expands the range of what can be automated and, at the same time, the range of failures that can occur without a visible error.
- `governance-definition` — the means by which an organization is directed and controlled.
- `governance-enabling` — governance sets guardrails that enable delivery, not merely restrict it.
- `direct-monitor-evaluate` — the governing activities: set direction, monitor performance, evaluate results.
- `governance-vs-management` — governance sets direction and holds accountable; management executes within it.
- `governing-body` — the group accountable for the organization's direction and control.
- `delegated-authority` — management operates under authority delegated by the governing body.
- `continual-improvement-model` — a structured, repeatable approach to improving services and practices.
- `ci-steps` — the model's stages, from vision through action to measurement and momentum.
- `ci-vision-to-action` — translating an improvement vision into concrete, measured steps.
- `ci-everyone-responsible` — continual improvement is a shared responsibility, not one team's job.
- `ci-application` — applying the model to a real improvement effort.
- `ci-common-failures` — typical breakdowns: no baseline, no clear vision, no follow-through.
- `ci-baseline-measurement` — measuring the starting point so improvement can be shown.
- `ci-for-ai-services` — an AI service is never "done": drift and changing data make continual measurement and improvement structural, not optional.

# D3 — The Service Lifecycle & Management Practices  *(17.5%)*

**Intent.** The operating layer: the product-and-service lifecycle activities (iterative,
not sequential), value streams, and the core management practices a practitioner must
place and use — incident, problem, change, request, service level, monitoring. Apply-
heavy, with one **diagnostic task (3.6)** — analyzing a recurring incident to its
root-cause problem, the core reasoning competence of service operations, at entry-tier
Analyze. **AI enters at the practices it genuinely changes**: a drop in AI output quality
is itself an incident (3.5), a model update is a change to be governed (3.7), and an AI
service must be monitored for drift, not just uptime (3.9). Task 3.11 is the bridge into
D4 — where AI **relocates** human work inside a practice without moving accountability. Task
3.12 adds **supplier and service-provider management** with its AISM-native edge — the
dependency risk of running a service on an AI model you do not own (lock-in, and a provider
changing the model underneath you). The lifecycle, practice definitions, value streams, the
root-cause skill itself, and the service desk are taught clean; D4 carries the AI-native
operations (AIOps, virtual agents, agentic automation) in depth.

- `acquire-and-build` — obtaining or constructing the components of a service.
- `transition-and-operate` — moving a service into live use and running it.
- `deliver-and-support` — providing the service to consumers and supporting its use.
- `lifecycle-activity-selection` — placing a real situation into the activity it belongs to.

- `practice-types` — practices grouped by focus (general management, service management, and technical/product areas).
- `general-vs-service-practices` — broad management practices vs those specific to running services.
- `value-stream` — a series of steps an organization uses to create and deliver products and services.

- `value-stream-purpose` — modeling how work actually flows so it can be improved.
- `incident-management` — restoring normal service operation as quickly as possible after an unplanned interruption.
- `problem-management` — finding and removing the underlying causes of incidents.
- `incident-vs-problem` — an incident is the disruption; a problem is the cause of one or more incidents.
- `known-error` — a problem whose cause and workaround are documented.
- `workaround` — a means of reducing or eliminating an incident's impact without a full fix.
- `ai-service-incident` — a degradation in an AI service's output quality is a service incident even when the system is technically available; "up" is not the same as "working".

- `change-enablement` — maximizing successful service changes by assessing and authorizing them appropriately.

- `change-authority` — the person or group empowered to authorize a given change.
- `ai-model-change` — updating or retraining a model is a change that can alter service behavior unpredictably, so it needs assessment and authorization like any change, with extra care for unforeseeable effects.
- `service-request-management` — handling pre-defined, user-initiated requests smoothly and consistently.
- `service-desk` — the single point of contact between the provider and its users.
- `request-vs-incident` — a request is a normal, planned demand; an incident is an unplanned disruption.
- `service-level-management` — setting, monitoring, and reviewing targets for service performance.
- `monitoring-and-event-management` — observing services and managing the events (state changes) they emit.
- `event-alert-threshold` — the level at which an observed event becomes an alert requiring attention.
- `monitoring-ai-services` — an AI service must be monitored for behavior and output-quality drift over time, not only for up/down availability.
- `value-stream-mapping` — visualizing the steps of a value stream to find delay, rework, and waste.
- `waste-identification` — spotting steps that add cost or delay without adding value.
- `flow-efficiency` — the proportion of end-to-end time in which value is actually being added.
- `ai-in-practices` — where AI is applied inside a management practice (e.g., triage, detection, assessment).
- `work-relocation` — AI moves *where* human effort is spent within a practice; it does not remove the practice.
- `human-accountability-retained` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — accountability for the outcome stays with a human even when AI performs a step.
- `ai-augmented-practice-boundary` — the line between what the AI does and what a human must still decide inside a practice.
- `practice-transformed-not-replaced` — AI changes *how* a practice is performed (where effort and judgment sit), but the practice, its purpose, and its accountability remain.
- `supplier-management` — coordinating the third parties and providers a service depends on, so their contribution is reliable.
- `service-provider-dependency` — a service's value can rest on providers the organization does not control; that dependency must be managed, not assumed.
- `ai-model-supplier-risk` — an AI service often runs on a foundation model owned by an external provider, concentrating critical dependency in a supplier.
- `ai-vendor-lock-in` — deep reliance on one model provider makes switching costly and slow, weakening the organization's position.
- `model-changed-underneath` — an external provider can update or deprecate a model, changing your service's behavior without your action; the dependency must be monitored and contracted for.

### D4 concepts

- `event-correlation` — grouping related events so many alerts collapse into one actionable issue.

- `predictive-service-management` — using data to anticipate issues before they cause disruption.
- `proactive-vs-reactive` — acting on prediction and prevention rather than only responding to failures.
- `failure-prediction` — forecasting a likely failure from patterns in operational data.

- `deflection-rate` — the share of contacts resolved without a human agent.
- `virtual-agent-limits` — where a virtual agent should stop: ambiguity, high stakes, low confidence.

- `confidence-threshold` — the certainty level below which the agent escalates rather than acts.
- `escalation-and-handoff` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — designed mechanisms for a human to take over or be brought in.
- `intelligent-automation` — automation that incorporates AI to handle variation and judgment, not just fixed rules.
- `scripted-vs-intelligent-automation` — deterministic scripts vs adaptive, model-assisted automation.
- `automation-in-workflows` — embedding automated steps within a service workflow.
- `agentic-service-workflow` — a workflow where an AI agent plans and takes multi-step service actions.
- `autonomy-levels` — how much an agent may decide and act without human approval.
- `action-reversibility` — whether an action can be undone, a key input to how much autonomy is safe.
- `reduced-per-step-intervention` — the defining trait of an agentic workflow: a human no longer approves each step, distinguishing it from step-by-step automation.
- `bounded-autonomy` — granting autonomy only within explicit, safe limits.
- `autonomy-decision-factors` — the inputs that set a safe autonomy level: reversibility, stakes, confidence, oversight cost.

- `human-in-the-loop` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — a human reviews and approves within the decision loop before action.
- `human-on-the-loop` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — a human supervises and can intervene without approving each action.
- `oversight-in-service-actions` — applying human oversight specifically to AI actions taken on live services.
- `risk-based-oversight` — matching the strength of oversight to the consequence of the action.
- `oversight-design` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — designing where and how humans oversee a deployed system.

- `operation-degradation-diagnosis` — reasoning from symptoms to the cause of a degraded AI operation.
- `model-drift` *(AIGRM-I bridge)* — degradation of a deployed model's performance as conditions shift.
- `data-quality-in-ops` — poor or shifted operational data as a driver of AIOps failure.
- `automation-failure-mode` — the characteristic ways an automated/agentic operation goes wrong.

### D6 concepts
- `service-experience` — the whole of how a user perceives and feels about interacting with a service, beyond whether it technically works.
- `experience-vs-performance` — a service can meet every technical target and still deliver a poor experience; the two are measured differently.
- `perception-and-value` — perceived value depends on experience, so experience is part of value, not a cosmetic extra.
- `experience-measurement` — measuring service quality from the user's perspective: did they achieve their goal, and how did it feel.
- `outcome-based-metric` — a measure of whether the user reached their intended outcome, not just whether a system was available.
- `beyond-technical-sla` — technical service levels can all be "green" while the user's experience is poor; experience needs its own measures (the generalization of the SLA "watermelon" pitfall).
- `experience-improvement` — choosing a change that raises the user's perceived experience, not just a technical metric.
- `friction-identification` — spotting the points where a service frustrates or slows the user.
- `experience-design-choice` — selecting the design change that most improves how the service feels to use.
- `trust-in-ai-service` — users co-create value with an AI service only if they trust it enough to rely on it, and rely on it appropriately.
- `appropriate-trust` — calibrated trust: neither over-trusting a fallible AI nor under-trusting a useful one; both destroy value.
- `adoption-prerequisite` — an AI service delivers no value if users will not adopt it; adoption is a service outcome to be earned.
- `trust-transparency` — being open about what the AI is, what it does well, and where it errs is what makes trust calibrated rather than blind.
- `building-appropriate-trust` — choosing actions (transparency, explainability, reliable escalation) that raise calibrated trust.
- `transparency-action` — disclosing AI use, confidence, and limits so users can judge when to rely on the service.
- `escalation-as-trust` — a visible, reliable path to a human raises trust in the AI by making its limits safe.
- `over-trust-correction` — designing against blind over-reliance, e.g., prompting users to verify high-stakes AI outputs.
- `sustainable-service` — delivering service value without disproportionate environmental or social cost, now and over time.
- `triple-dimension-value` — value has environmental and social dimensions alongside the economic one; sustainability weighs all three.
- `sustainability-in-value-system` — sustainability is woven into how value is created, not treated as an isolated checklist.
- `ai-compute-footprint` — running AI services consumes significant compute and energy, giving them a material environmental cost.
- `green-ai` — reducing the energy and resource cost of AI services through efficient models and operations.
- `efficiency-as-service-quality` — compute efficiency is a service-management concern, not only a cost line: it affects sustainability and scalability.
- `sustainability-tradeoff` — balancing a lower footprint against service quality, cost, and outcomes.
- `right-sizing-model` — using a model no larger than the task needs, cutting waste without hurting outcomes.
- `efficient-inference` — operational choices (caching, batching, smaller models for simple cases) that cut compute per request.
- `sustainability-without-degradation` — choosing the footprint reduction that preserves the user's outcome and experience.
