## About this document

This is the **certification scheme** for the AI Governance & Risk Management I
credential: the published contract describing what the credential certifies, how a
candidate earns it, and how Certidemy keeps that decision fair, consistent, and
defensible. It is written to align with the structure of the ISO/IEC 17024
framework for bodies operating certification of persons.

**A note on accreditation status.** Certidemy is **not currently an accredited
certification body**. This scheme is designed to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework —
"audit-ready by design" — so that the credential is structurally legitimate now
and the body can pursue formal accreditation once operational history and
candidate data exist. Where a requirement depends on data or governance that only
accumulates after launch, this document says so plainly rather than overstating
readiness.

**A note on subject matter.** This credential concerns *AI governance and risk
management* — the organizational discipline of governing AI systems responsibly.
The body of knowledge draws on public, citable frameworks (the NIST AI Risk
Management Framework, the EU AI Act, ISO/IEC 42001 and the related ISO AI-standards
family, the OECD AI Principles, and comparable published instruments). Certidemy
teaches the *structure and reasoning* of these frameworks in its own words; it does
not reproduce their normative text, and it does not present itself as, or as
endorsed by, any of these standards bodies or regulators. See the framework-identity
line in §11.

## 2. Purpose and scope

The AIGRM-I credential certifies that the holder understands how to **govern AI
systems and manage AI risk** in an organizational setting: what AI governance is
and why it is distinct from general IT or data governance, how to identify,
analyze, evaluate, and treat the risks specific to AI (including the risks unique to
generative and agentic systems), how the major AI governance instruments
(voluntary frameworks, binding regulation, and management-system standards) fit
together, how accountability is assigned across the AI lifecycle and value chain,
and how a responsible-AI function operates in practice.

The distinguishing scope of this credential is that it is **framework-literate but
not framework-bound**: it teaches the *shared structure* of AI governance — risk
processes, trustworthy-AI characteristics, lifecycle accountability, the
provider-vs-deployer split, human oversight, documentation and monitoring — rather
than certifying mastery of any single regulation or standard. The signature
competency is **accountable AI deployment across the lifecycle**: knowing who is
answerable for what, at which stage, and why accountability for AI-assisted
decisions cannot be delegated to the system.

**Target audience.** Practitioners responsible for, or moving into, AI governance,
risk, compliance, audit, or responsible-AI roles; product, legal, security, and
data-protection professionals whose work now includes AI oversight; and technical
staff who need a working command of the governance and risk frame around the
systems they build or deploy.

**Out of scope.** This credential does not certify legal practice or the giving of
legal advice on any specific regulation; the engineering or development of AI/ML
systems; data-science or model-building proficiency; or jurisdiction-specific
compliance sign-off. It certifies governance and risk-management literacy and
applied judgment, not regulatory authority.

## 3. Eligibility

The AIGRM-I examination operates under **open eligibility**: there are no mandatory
prerequisites, formal training hours, or prior-credential requirements to attempt
the examination. This is an entry-tier ("I") credential, and the body has chosen
open access as a deliberate scheme policy.

Candidates are strongly advised to complete the associated body-of-knowledge
coursework (or to possess equivalent practical experience) before attempting the
secure examination, but completion of Certidemy coursework is **not** a condition
of eligibility and confers no advantage in the secure examination (§8).

## 4. Body of knowledge

The body of knowledge is organized into **5 domains** comprising **49 assessable
tasks**, supported by **165 defined concepts** and delivered through **5
instructional modules / 49 lessons** (authored in English; see the open content
note in §11).

The five domains and their examination weightings:

| Domain | Title | Weight | Tasks |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Foundations of AI Governance & Trustworthy AI | 15.0% | 8 |
| D2 | AI Risk Management in Practice | 22.5% | 11 |
| D3 | Regulatory & Standards Landscape | 20.0% | 10 |
| D4 | The AI Lifecycle & Accountable Deployment | 25.0% | 12 |
| D5 | Responsible AI, Ethics & the Governance Function | 17.5% | 8 |

(Domain titles, weights, and task counts are reconciled against the live `domains`
and `tasks` rows for AIGRM-I; weights sum to 100 and per-domain task counts sum to
49.)

All **49 tasks are within examination scope.** AI-governance competencies are
distributed across all five domains, with the heaviest weighting (D4) on lifecycle
accountability and accountable deployment — the scheme's signature area.

The body of knowledge is grounded in public, citable AI-governance instruments
(the NIST AI RMF, the EU AI Act, the ISO/IEC 42001 management-system standard and
the wider ISO AI-standards family, the OECD AI Principles, and comparable published
frameworks) and in established risk-management practice. It does not depend on any
third-party vendor's product, any single regulation, or any specific AI tool, and
it teaches the structure and reasoning of these instruments in Certidemy's own
words rather than reproducing their normative text (§11).

## 5. Competency model and job-task basis

The domain-and-task structure is a **job-task model**: each of the 49 tasks
describes what a competent AI-governance practitioner must know or do, and each task
is linked to the concepts it draws on and to the assessment items that test it.
This produces a complete, queryable **traceability matrix** from domain → task →
concept → assessment item → instructional lesson (§10).

**Validation status — stated honestly.** The competency model was derived from the
public AI-governance frameworks named above, established risk-management practice,
and analysis of the competencies the responsible-AI function now requires.
**Independent subject-matter-expert (SME) panel validation of the job-task analysis
is planned but not yet completed**, and is a prerequisite for accreditation. (An
external reviewer pass was performed on the job-task analysis and on the
instructional content during authoring; that is editorial / second-reviewer rigor,
not the formal SME job-task validation the standard requires.)

## 7. Pass mark and standard-setting

**Current pass mark:** 80% (64 of 80 items).

**Methodology — stated honestly.** The pass mark is **criterion-referenced**: it
reflects the level of mastery the body judges necessary to certify competence, not
a curve relative to other candidates. The current 80% threshold is a **provisional,
expert-judgment cut score** set at the "I"-tier standard.

It has **not yet** been validated by a formal standard-setting study, because
defensible standard-setting (e.g., a modified-Angoff panel and statistical
confirmation against candidate performance) **requires real candidate data that does
not yet exist.** The body's plan is to collect candidate response data through live
operation, compute item-level statistics and form reliability, then convene a
standard-setting panel to confirm or adjust the cut score against that evidence.
Until then, the 80% pass mark is held as the documented, criterion-referenced
standard and applied uniformly to every candidate and form.

## 8. Assessment integrity and separation of functions

**Separation of training and certification.** Certidemy operates a **technical
separation** between the learning function and the certification function through a
strict item-pool firewall:

- The **practice pool** (used by the adaptive learning engine and study tools) and
  the **secure pool** (used only to assemble the live certification examination) are
  entirely distinct sets of items.
- Secure items are **never linked into the practice/learning graph**
  (`question_concepts`), so no secure examination content is reachable through
  study, review, or practice. The secure examination is **inert to the adaptive
  learning engine.** This is a DB-provable invariant: a firewall query returns zero
  secure items carrying a concept link (see §10).
- Completion of coursework confers **no advantage** on the secure examination and is
  not a condition of eligibility (§3).

**Governance-level separation** (an impartiality structure ensuring certification
decisions are free from commercial or training-side influence) is a formal
accreditation requirement on the body's roadmap; it is named here as
established-in-design, to-be-formalized-operationally.

**Item bank inventory.** The item bank holds, **per assessable task**, a floor of 8
secure items and 10 practice items **per language** across en / es-419 / pt-BR, for
all 49 tasks. Current holdings (some tasks may exceed the floor, which is benign
over-coverage):

| Pool | Floor per task per language | Design total (49×) | Current holdings |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure (examination) | 8 | 1,176 | 1,176 (392 × 3 languages, verified) |
| Practice (learning) | 10 | 1,470 | 1,470 (490 × 3 languages, verified) |

Both banks are complete and balanced at exactly their floor across all 49 tasks in
all three languages: **secure 392 per language** (49 × 8), **practice 490 per
language** (49 × 10). Verified by the completion-gate, balance, and coverage queries
(§10, §12): every practice item is concept-linked (engine-reachable), every secure
item is firewall-isolated (zero links into the practice graph), and every question
group holds exactly its three language rows.

**Form variation.** The secure bank provides substantial over-coverage of every
domain's blueprint quota — every domain sits well above its per-form quota in each
language — so forms vary candidate-to-candidate and no single form approaches
exposure of the bank.

### 8.1 Answer-cue neutrality (measured and corrected)

A multiple-choice item must be answerable **only by knowing the content** — not by
exploiting surface cues a test-wise candidate can read without competence. This
scheme's item bank was generated under an explicit answer-cue-neutrality control and
is reproducibly auditable.

**The control.** A single shared cue-neutrality module governs both generators. For
every item, before translation, it (a) injects length-parity / no-positional-habit /
no-rhetorical-tell rules into the authoring prompt, (b) audits the item and drops or
repairs any whose key dominates on length or shows the absolute-word tell (every
distractor carrying an absolute — always / never / only — while the key hedges), and
(c) deterministically shuffles option order (Fisher–Yates) so the correct answer
lands in a uniformly random slot. Cues are neutralized on the English skeleton, so
all three languages inherit a clean item. The control is identical to the one
measured on the SD-AI-I bank; during AIGRM-I generation it actively dropped
absolute-word-tell and length-dominant items and normalized option lengths (visible
in the generation logs).

**The result (AIGRM-I secure pool).** The cue-audit metrics are computed by the same
cue-audit query used for SD-AI-I and are reproducible at any time against the live
secure pool. Measured on the authored English skeleton (392 items):

| Correct-answer slot | Items | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | 99 | 25.3% |
| 2nd | 97 | 24.7% |
| 3rd | 88 | 22.4% |
| 4th | 108 | 27.6% |

The correct answer is distributed essentially uniformly across the four option
positions (target ~25% per slot), so a candidate cannot improve their odds by
favoring a position. Length-based tells were controlled during generation (the
cue-guard drops items whose key dominates on length and normalizes option lengths
within the permitted spread), and the shuffle is deterministic Fisher–Yates, so the
three languages inherit the same clean, position-neutral skeleton.

**Honest ceiling.** This control proves the item bank is **cue-neutral and
structurally sound**. It does **not** establish item difficulty or discrimination,
which can only come from live candidate response data and remain pending (§7).

**Credential verification.** Every issued credential carries a unique credential
code and a public verification record, allowing any third party to confirm a
credential's authenticity, holder, issue date, and validity status.

## 9. Recertification

AIGRM-I credentials are valid for **1 year** from issuance.

**Rationale.** The credential certifies competence in AI governance and risk
management — a domain whose regulatory landscape, standards, and system
capabilities (particularly generative and agentic AI) are changing rapidly. A
1-year cycle keeps a current credential a genuine signal of current competence.
Recertification re-tests against the then-current body of knowledge, which is
itself reviewed as the underlying frameworks evolve (§11).

## 10. Traceability and coverage

The scheme maintains a complete, queryable traceability matrix:

**domain → task → concept → assessment item**, and
**concept → instructional lesson**

Three audit-relevant guarantees follow, each backed by a live database view or query
(these are the intended data feeds for the super-admin governance dashboard; see
§12):

1. **Coverage** — every one of the 49 in-scope tasks is supported by assessment
   items in both pools, in all three languages (verified: every task carries ≥8
   secure and ≥10 practice items per language), and is taught by at least one
   lesson. Proven live: **165 / 165 concepts taught, 165 / 165 concepts tested, 0
   taught-not-yet-practiced.**
2. **No untaught testing** — every concept assessed is present in the published body
   of knowledge, and every examination item resolves back to the lesson(s) that
   teach its concept(s). Proven live: **untaught-testing violations = 0.** This is
   the ISO/IEC 17024 "no untaught testing" guarantee, queryable via the coverage
   view.
3. **Secure firewall** — the secure examination pool carries **zero** links into the
   practice/learning graph, provable by a firewall query returning 0 (verified this
   build; see §8).

**A note on what the coverage view measures.** The coverage view derives "tested"
from the practice/learning graph (`question_concepts`). Because secure items carry
no such link by design (the firewall), secure-pool sufficiency is proven not by the
coverage view but by the blueprint allocator plus a per-task count (every task at its
≥8-per-language floor, confirmed). Two pools, two proofs: practice reach by the
coverage view, secure sufficiency by the allocator and count.

The projection of lessons into this matrix, and the generation of assessment items
against the taught concepts, are maintained by automated, re-runnable controls
rather than by hand, so the matrix stays complete as content evolves.

## 11. Scheme maintenance and governance

**Review cadence.** Reviewed when a governing framework in the body of knowledge is
materially revised (e.g., an EU AI Act implementing act, a new ISO/IEC 42001
edition, a NIST AI RMF update), when the job-task analysis is re-validated, or at
minimum annually.

**Change control.** Changes to domains, weightings, the item bank, or the pass mark
are versioned and recorded.

**Framework-identity line (scope discipline).** The named public frameworks (NIST AI
RMF, EU AI Act, ISO/IEC 42001 and the ISO AI-standards family, OECD AI Principles,
etc.) are used as the **foundational body of knowledge** and named in lesson content
as pedagogical reference. They are **not** claimed as Certidemy's own, and this
credential is **not** presented as, equivalent to, or endorsed by any of these
standards bodies or regulators. The credential certifies a candidate's governance
and risk literacy; it is not a statement of legal or regulatory compliance.

**Open content note.** The examination and practice item banks are (to be) complete
in all three languages. Instructional lessons are fully authored and complete in
English; full localization of every lesson into es-419 and pt-BR is not yet started.
A candidate may therefore sit the examination and practice in any of the three
languages once the credential is published, while lesson study material localizes
progressively. This is stated transparently rather than implied as fully complete.

**Publication status.** This credential is **pre-launch** at the time of writing
(`status = coming_soon`): the teaching layer is complete and coverage-proven, and
both the secure and practice item banks are complete, balanced, and verified
(firewall-isolated secure pool, concept-linked practice pool, 0 taught-not-yet-
practiced). Publication to `available` follows once lesson localization into es-419
and pt-BR is completed and the launch checklist is cleared.

**Open items on the path to accreditation** (named transparently):

- Independent SME-panel validation of the job-task analysis and weightings (§5).
- Formal standard-setting study and psychometric validation of the cut score, using
  candidate data collected in live operation (§7).
- Item difficulty and discrimination statistics from live candidate data (§8.1).
- Governance-level impartiality structure separating the certification decision from
  commercial and training functions (§8).
- Operational records required by the standard: appeals, complaints handling,
  examination-integrity / identity procedures, and internal audit.
- Practice item bank filled to floor; full lesson localization into es-419 and pt-BR
  (§11, above).

The credential is positioned as **"built to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework /
audit-ready by design,"** not as accredited, until accreditation is formally
achieved.
