# Certification Scheme — Scrum Master I — AI (SM-AI-I)

**Scheme owner:** Certidemy (the certification body)
**Scheme code:** SM-AI-I
**Document version:** 1.0
**Status:** Active scheme of record
**Last updated:** June 2026

---

## About this document

This is the **certification scheme** for the Scrum Master I — AI credential: the
published contract describing what the credential certifies, how a candidate
earns it, and how Certidemy keeps that decision fair, consistent, and
defensible. It is written to align with the structure of the ISO/IEC 17024
framework for bodies operating certification of persons.

**A note on accreditation status.** Certidemy is **not currently an accredited
certification body**. This scheme is designed to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework —
"audit-ready by design" — so that the credential is structurally legitimate now
and the body can pursue formal accreditation once operational history and
candidate data exist. Where a requirement depends on data or governance that
only accumulates after launch, this document says so plainly rather than
overstating readiness. Honesty about what is established versus provisional is
itself part of operating to the standard.

## 2. Purpose and scope

The SM-AI-I credential certifies that the holder understands the Scrum framework
as defined by the 2020 Scrum Guide **and** can apply that understanding in a team
context where AI tooling is part of how work gets done.

The distinguishing scope of this credential is that AI fluency is **not a
bolt-on**. Rather than isolating AI into a single appendix domain, the scheme
weaves AI-relevant competencies through every domain of Scrum Master practice:
protecting empiricism when AI accelerates output, distinguishing AI tools from
team members and the accountabilities that cannot be delegated to them, applying
the Definition of Done to AI-generated work, using AI-derived signal as input to
inspection without ceding the team's decision-making, and coaching a team back to
empiricism when AI-augmentation anti-patterns appear.

**Target audience.** Practicing or aspiring Scrum Masters, Agile team members,
and team leads working in environments where AI tooling is in active use.

**Out of scope.** This credential does not certify software engineering ability,
AI/ML system development, or organizational change-management beyond the team and
immediate organizational context.

---

## 3. Eligibility

The SM-AI-I examination operates under **open eligibility**: there are no
mandatory prerequisites, formal training hours, or prior-credential requirements
to attempt the examination. This is an entry-tier ("I") credential, and the body
has chosen open access as a deliberate scheme policy.

Candidates are strongly advised to complete the associated body-of-knowledge
coursework (or to possess equivalent practical experience) before attempting the
secure examination, but completion of Certidemy coursework is **not** a condition
of eligibility and confers no advantage in the secure examination (see §8,
Assessment integrity — the training and certification functions are separated).

---

## 4. Body of knowledge

The body of knowledge is organized into **5 domains** comprising **51 assessable
tasks**, supported by **109 defined concepts** and delivered through **5
instructional modules / 31 lessons** (authored in all three credential
languages).

The five domains and their examination weightings:

| Domain | Title | Weight | Tasks |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Agile Foundations & Empirical Thinking | 12.5% | 7 |
| D2 | The Scrum Team & Accountabilities | 22.5% | 11 |
| D3 | Scrum Events | 25.0% | 11 |
| D4 | Scrum Artifacts & Commitments | 17.5% | 11 |
| D5 | Scrum Master in Practice & Organizational Context | 22.5% | 11 |

All **51 tasks are within examination scope.** AI-specific competencies are
distributed across all five domains rather than concentrated, consistent with the
scheme's purpose (§2).

The authoritative reference for Scrum content is the **2020 Scrum Guide**
(Schwaber & Sutherland). AI-related tasks are grounded in the concept definitions
of the body of knowledge and in established Agile practice; they do not depend on
any third-party vendor's product or any specific AI tool.

---

## 5. Competency model and job-task basis

The domain-and-task structure is a **job-task model**: each domain decomposes
into discrete tasks describing what a competent Scrum Master must know or do, and
each task is linked to the concepts it draws on and to the assessment items that
test it. This produces a complete, queryable **traceability matrix** from domain
→ task → concept → assessment item → instructional lesson (see §10).

**Validation status — stated honestly.** The competency model was derived from
the 2020 Scrum Guide, established Scrum Master practice, and analysis of the
competencies introduced by AI-augmented team contexts. **Independent
subject-matter-expert (SME) panel validation of the job-task analysis is
planned but not yet completed.** Formal SME sign-off on the task list and
weightings is a prerequisite for accreditation and is on the body's roadmap; it
is named here rather than implied as done.

## 6. Examination structure

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Number of items | 80 |
| Duration | 60 minutes |
| Item format | Single-best-answer multiple choice and true/false |
| Delivery language | Candidate-selected: en, es-419, or pt-BR |
| Scoring | Dichotomous (correct / incorrect); no negative marking |

**Blueprint (item allocation by domain).** Each examination form draws items
proportionally to the domain weightings, yielding the following target
distribution at 80 items:

| Domain | Weight | Items per form (target) |
|---|---|---|
| D1 | 12.5% | 10 |
| D2 | 22.5% | 18 |
| D3 | 25.0% | 20 |
| D4 | 17.5% | 14 |
| D5 | 22.5% | 18 |

The examination assembler draws each form from the secure item bank according to
this blueprint and **refuses to issue a form if any domain is short of its
quota**, guaranteeing every candidate receives a blueprint-valid form.

## 7. Pass mark and standard-setting

**Current pass mark:** 80% (64 of 80 items).

**Methodology — stated honestly.** The pass mark is **criterion-referenced**: it
reflects the level of mastery the body judges necessary to certify competence,
not a curve relative to other candidates. The current 80% threshold is a
**provisional, expert-judgment cut score** set at the "I"-tier standard.

It has **not yet** been validated by a formal standard-setting study, because
defensible standard-setting (e.g., a modified-Angoff panel, and statistical
confirmation against candidate performance) **requires real candidate data that
does not yet exist.** The body's plan is to:

1. Collect candidate response data through live operation;
2. Compute item-level statistics (difficulty, discrimination) and form
   reliability;
3. Convene a standard-setting panel to confirm or adjust the cut score against
   that evidence.

Until that study is conducted, the 80% pass mark is held as the body's
documented, criterion-referenced standard, and is applied **uniformly** to every
candidate and every form. Treating the cut score as provisional-pending-data is
the honest position; a claim of psychometric validation prior to having
candidates would be false.

## 8. Assessment integrity and separation of functions

**Separation of training and certification.** ISO/IEC 17024 requires that the
function delivering training be separated from the function making the
certification decision. Certidemy operates a **technical separation** between the
two through a strict item-pool firewall:

- The **practice pool** (used by the adaptive learning engine and study tools) and
  the **secure pool** (used only to assemble the live certification examination)
  are entirely distinct sets of items.
- Secure items are **never linked into the practice/learning graph**, so no secure
  examination content is reachable through study, review, or practice. The secure
  examination is **inert to the adaptive learning engine** — sitting it produces no
  signal that the learning system can use, and the learning system can never
  surface a secure item.
- Completion of coursework confers **no advantage** on the secure examination and
  is not a condition of eligibility (§3).

This firewall is the body's current technical evidence of separation of
functions. **Governance-level separation** (an impartiality structure ensuring
certification decisions are free from commercial or training-side influence) is a
formal accreditation requirement and is on the body's roadmap; it is named here
as established-in-design, to-be-formalized-operationally.

**Item bank inventory (per language).**

| Pool | English | es-419 | pt-BR |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure (examination) | 408 | 408 | 408 |
| Practice (learning) | 619 | 609 | 609 |

**Form variation.** The secure bank provides substantial over-coverage of every
domain's blueprint quota — for example, Domain D3 (20 items per form) draws from
88 secure items per language; the smallest domain draws roughly 5–6× its
per-form quota. This margin ensures forms vary candidate-to-candidate and that no
single form approaches exposure of the bank.

**Credential verification.** Every issued credential carries a unique credential
code and a public verification record, allowing any third party to confirm a
credential's authenticity, holder, issue date, and validity status.

## 9. Recertification

SM-AI-I credentials are valid for **1 year** from issuance.

**Rationale.** The credential certifies competence in Scrum *as practiced in
AI-augmented teams* — a domain whose tools and practices are changing rapidly. A
1-year cycle (shorter than the 3-year or unlimited terms common to legacy Scrum
credentials) keeps a current credential a genuine signal of current competence,
rather than a one-time event whose relevance decays. Recertification re-tests
against the then-current body of knowledge.

## 10. Traceability and coverage

The scheme maintains a complete, queryable traceability matrix:

**domain → task → concept → assessment item**, and
**concept → instructional lesson**

Every assessable task links to the concepts it covers; every examination and
practice item links to the concepts it tests; every concept links to the
lesson(s) that teach it. This yields two audit-relevant guarantees:

1. **Coverage** — every in-scope task is supported by assessment items in both
   pools, in all three languages. (At time of writing: every task carries secure
   and practice items across en / es-419 / pt-BR.)
2. **No untaught testing** — every concept assessed on the examination is present
   in the published body of knowledge, and every examination item resolves back
   to the lesson(s) that teach its concept(s). This review-to-source link is the
   same mechanism candidates use to study from their results.

The projection of lessons into this matrix is maintained by an automated,
re-runnable control rather than by hand, so the matrix stays complete as content
evolves.

### 10.4 Trilingual equivalence and terminology control

This credential is delivered in English, Latin American Spanish (es-419) and
Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR). The published blueprint in each language states
what the credential attests, so a translated task statement that describes a
different competence than the exam measures would be invalid for every candidate
sitting in that language.

Terminology is governed by **`TERMINOLOGY-POLICY.md`**, the single source of
truth for all Certidemy credentials. It records sixteen rules, each traceable to
a primary source (the official EU AI Act language versions, the official
translated Scrum Guides, the ISO/IEC standards) or to a recorded external-review
decision.

Scrum roles and artifacts stay in English in all three languages (Rule 4). This is not an internal convention: the official Latin American Spanish and Brazilian Portuguese editions of the Scrum Guide do exactly this, and the Brazilian edition states explicitly that it uses the English word *developers* to simplify rather than to exclude. The Guide's own title **is** translated - `la Guia de Scrum` / `o Guia do Scrum` (Rule 5) - because a document name is neither a role nor an artifact. *Servant leadership* is `liderazgo de servicio` (Rule 6).

**Equivalence is enforced mechanically, not by procedure.** A database trigger
(`trg_invalidate_task_translations`) flips a task's translations back to
provisional in the same transaction whenever its English statement changes, and
the conformance gate (`verify-cert.mjs`, invariant `i18n.approved`) fails any
certification holding a provisional translation. The English cannot move without
the translations declaring themselves stale, and the credential cannot publish
while they are.

## 11. Scheme maintenance and governance

**Review cadence.** The scheme is reviewed when the underlying Scrum Guide is
revised, when the body's job-task analysis is re-validated, or at minimum
annually.

**Change control.** Changes to domains, weightings, the item bank, or the pass
mark are versioned and recorded.

**Open items on the path to accreditation** (named transparently):

- Independent SME-panel validation of the job-task analysis and weightings (§5).
- Formal standard-setting study and psychometric validation of the cut score,
  using candidate data collected in live operation (§7).
- Governance-level impartiality structure separating the certification decision
  from commercial and training functions (§8).
- Operational records required by the standard: candidate appeals process,
  complaints handling, examination-integrity / identity procedures, and internal
  audit.

These are the genuine prerequisites for accreditation. They depend on operating
history and a governance build-out, and are listed so that the body's position is
never overstated. The credential is positioned as **"built to the ISO/IEC 17024
framework / audit-ready by design,"** not as accredited, until accreditation is
formally achieved.
