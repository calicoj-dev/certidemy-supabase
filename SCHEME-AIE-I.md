## About this document

This is the **certification scheme** for the AI Essentials I credential: the
published contract describing what the credential certifies, how a candidate earns
it, and how Certidemy keeps that decision fair, consistent, and defensible. It is
written to align with the structure of the ISO/IEC 17024 framework for bodies
operating certification of persons.

**A note on accreditation status.** Certidemy is **not currently an accredited
certification body**. This scheme is designed to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework —
"audit-ready by design" — so that the credential is structurally legitimate now and
the body can pursue formal accreditation once operational history and candidate data
exist. Where a requirement depends on data or governance that only accumulates after
launch, this document says so plainly rather than overstating readiness.

Every quantitative claim in this document was measured against the live database at
the time of writing and is reproducible by re-running the queries named in §12.

## 2. Purpose and scope

The AIE-I credential certifies that the holder possesses **practical AI literacy for
the workplace**: that they can understand what everyday AI tools are and are not,
work with them effectively, judge their output, and use them safely and responsibly
in an organizational setting.

The distinguishing scope of this credential is that it is written **for
non-technical professionals**. It assumes no engineering, data-science, or
product-craft background. Scenarios are drawn from ordinary office work — drafting
and summarizing, answering routine questions, screening applications, checking a
figure before it is published — and the competencies tested are the ones that
determine whether AI is used well or badly by an ordinary employee.

The through-line is **capable but not authoritative**: AI can draft, summarize,
explain, and suggest, but it predicts plausible text rather than retrieving verified
truth. The holder is therefore certified as able to recognize what a tool can do,
detect when its confident output is wrong, verify before relying on it, protect
confidential data, recognize bias, and keep a human accountable for decisions that
affect people.

**Target audience.** Professionals in HR, marketing, sales, operations, finance,
administration, education, and team leadership — anyone who uses AI tools as part of
ordinary work and is accountable for how they are used.

**Out of scope.** This credential does not certify the ability to build, train,
fine-tune, deploy, or evaluate AI systems; it does not certify AI governance, risk
management, or compliance practice at a practitioner level (see AIGRM-I); it does
not certify proficiency in any specific vendor product; and it does not certify
technical or engineering competence of any kind.

**Relationship to the EU AI Act.** Article 4 of the EU AI Act establishes an
expectation that organizations ensure a sufficient level of AI literacy among staff
who use AI systems. AIE-I is designed as a **structured, per-individual, verifiable
record of workplace AI literacy** that an organization may hold as part of the
evidence of measures it has taken. It is stated precisely: AIE-I is **not** a
compliance certification, does **not** guarantee compliance with the EU AI Act or
any other instrument, and is **not** "certified under Article 4." No such claim is
made in this scheme, in marketing, or in the instructional content.

## 3. Eligibility

The AIE-I examination operates under **open eligibility**: there are no mandatory
prerequisites, formal training hours, or prior-credential requirements to attempt the
examination. This is a literacy-tier credential and the body has chosen open access
as a deliberate scheme policy — the credential exists precisely to reach
professionals with no prior AI background.

Candidates are advised to complete the associated body-of-knowledge coursework (or to
possess equivalent practical familiarity) before attempting the secure examination,
but completion of Certidemy coursework is **not** a condition of eligibility and
confers no advantage in the secure examination (§8).

## 4. Body of knowledge

The body of knowledge is organized into **3 domains** comprising **16 assessable
tasks**, supported by **47 defined concepts** and delivered through **3 instructional
modules / 16 lessons**, complete in all three languages (48 lesson records).

The three domains and their examination weightings:

| Domain | Title | Weight | Tasks |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | AI Concepts & Landscape | 40% | 6 |
| D2 | Working with Generative AI | 36% | 6 |
| D3 | Responsible & Safe Use at Work | 24% | 4 |

All **16 tasks are within examination scope.**

The body of knowledge is grounded in the concept definitions of the scheme itself and
in established, vendor-neutral practice for the use of generative AI at work. It does
not depend on any third-party vendor's product, any specific AI tool, or any
proprietary framework.

## 5. Competency model and job-task basis

The domain-and-task structure is a **job-task model**: each of the 16 tasks describes
what an AI-literate professional must know or do, and each task is linked to the
concepts it draws on and to the assessment items that test it. This produces a
complete, queryable **traceability matrix** from domain → task → concept → assessment
item → instructional lesson (§10). The model holds **47 task-to-concept links** with
no concept unassigned and no task unsupported.

**Validation status — stated honestly.** The competency model was derived from
analysis of the competencies required to use generative AI tools safely and
effectively in a non-technical professional role, and from the literacy expectations
emerging in regulation (§2). **Independent subject-matter-expert (SME) panel
validation of the job-task analysis is planned but not yet completed**, and is a
prerequisite for accreditation. An external AI reviewer pass was performed on the
job-task analysis and on the instructional content during authoring; that is
editorial and structural review, **not** the formal SME job-task validation the
standard requires, and it is not presented as such.

## 6. Examination structure

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Number of items | 25 |
| Duration | 30 minutes |
| Pass mark | 80% (20 of 25 items) |
| Item format | Single-best-answer multiple choice and true/false |
| Delivery language | Candidate-selected: en, es-419, or pt-BR |
| Scoring | Dichotomous (correct / incorrect); no negative marking |

**Blueprint — item allocation by domain.** Each form draws items proportionally to the
domain weightings (largest-remainder rounding, so parts sum exactly to 25):

| Domain | Weight | Items per form |
|---|---|---|
| D1 | 40% | 10 |
| D2 | 36% | 9 |
| D3 | 24% | 6 |

**Blueprint — cognitive level.** This is a **literacy-tier** credential, and its
cognitive ceiling is a scheme commitment, not an accident of authoring. The
examination tests at Bloom levels 1–3 only:

| Cognitive level | Target | Items per form |
|---|---|---|
| Remember | 44% | 11 |
| Understand | 40% | 10 |
| Apply | 16% | 4 |

**No item in the secure examination pool tests above Apply.** This is enforced at two
independent points: the item generator is constrained to the tier's ceiling and
cannot emit a higher-level item, and the form assembler stratifies each form to the
declared mix. Measured on the live secure pool: **0 items at Analyze or above.**

The examination assembler draws each form from the secure bank according to **both**
the domain weighting and the cognitive-level mix, and **refuses to issue a form if any
domain is short of its quota**, guaranteeing every candidate receives a
blueprint-valid form. Conformance is therefore structural rather than incidental: the
form a candidate sits matches the blueprint this document publishes.

**Pool sufficiency for the blueprint** (secure pool, per language):

| Domain | Form seats | Needs (R/U/A) | Available (R/U/A) | Minimum headroom |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | 10 | 4 / 4 / 2 | 16 / 26 / 6 | 3.0× |
| D2 | 9 | 4 / 4 / 1 | 11 / 30 / 7 | 2.8× |
| D3 | 6 | 3 / 2 / 1 | 8 / 21 / 3 | 2.7× |

Every cognitive band in every domain holds at least 2.7× the items a single form
requires, so forms vary candidate-to-candidate without exhausting any band.

## 7. Pass mark and standard-setting

**Current pass mark:** 80% (20 of 25 items).

**Methodology — stated honestly.** The pass mark is **criterion-referenced**: it
reflects the level of mastery the body judges necessary to certify competence, not a
curve relative to other candidates. The current 80% threshold is a **provisional,
expert-judgment cut score** set at the literacy-tier standard.

It has **not yet** been validated by a formal standard-setting study, because
defensible standard-setting (e.g., a modified-Angoff panel and statistical
confirmation against candidate performance) **requires real candidate data that does
not yet exist.** The body's plan is to collect candidate response data through live
operation, compute item-level statistics and form reliability, then convene a
standard-setting panel to confirm or adjust the cut score against that evidence.
Until then, the 80% pass mark is held as the documented, criterion-referenced
standard and applied uniformly to every candidate and form.

## 8. Assessment integrity and separation of functions

**Separation of training and certification.** Certidemy operates a **technical
separation** between the learning function and the certification function through a
strict item-pool firewall:

- The **practice pool** (used by the adaptive learning engine and study tools) and the
  **secure pool** (used only to assemble the live certification examination) are
  entirely distinct sets of items.
- Secure items are **never linked into the practice/learning graph**
  (`question_concepts`), so no secure examination content is reachable through study,
  review, or practice. The secure examination is **inert to the adaptive learning
  engine.** This is a DB-provable invariant: the firewall query returns **0** secure
  items carrying a concept link (§10).
- Completion of coursework confers **no advantage** on the secure examination and is
  not a condition of eligibility (§3).

**Governance-level separation** (an impartiality structure ensuring certification
decisions are free from commercial or training-side influence) is a formal
accreditation requirement on the body's roadmap; it is named here as
established-in-design, to-be-formalized-operationally.

**Item bank inventory.** The item bank holds, **per assessable task**, a floor of 8
secure items and 10 practice items **per language** across en / es-419 / pt-BR, for
all 16 tasks. Both floors are met exactly, with no task short:

| Pool | Floor per task per language | Design total (16 tasks × 3 langs) | Current holdings |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure (examination) | 8 | 384 | **384** |
| Practice (learning) | 10 | 480 | **480** |

**Trilingual integrity.** Every question exists as exactly three language records
sharing one group identifier. Measured: **0 question groups holding other than 3
language rows.** Cue neutralization is applied to the English skeleton before
translation, so all three languages inherit an identical, clean item.

### 8.1 Answer-cue neutrality (measured)

A multiple-choice item must be answerable **only by knowing the content** — not by
exploiting surface cues a test-wise candidate can read without competence. This
scheme's item bank is governed by a shared cue-neutrality control and the result is
measured and reproducible.

**The control.** For every item, before translation, the control (a) injects
length-parity, no-positional-habit, and no-rhetorical-tell rules into the authoring
prompt; (b) audits each item and repairs or drops any whose key dominates on length
or shows the absolute-word tell (correct answer hedged, distractors carrying
always/never/only); and (c) deterministically shuffles option order (Fisher–Yates) so
the correct answer lands in a uniformly random slot.

**The result (AIE-I secure pool, measured at n = 128 English items).**

| Metric | Chance baseline | AIE-I secure (n=128) | Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Correct-answer position 1 / 2 / 3 / 4 | 25% each | 19% / 32% / 24% / 25% | ~25% each |
| Correct answer is strict-longest option | ~25% | 44.5% | ~25–45% |
| **Mean margin when key is longest** | — | **5.1 characters** | marginal, non-diagnostic |
| **Key longest by a visible margin (≥20 chars)** | — | **0.8% (1 of 128)** | ≤ ~1% |

**Reading these figures honestly.** The strict-longest rate (44.5%) is higher than the
professional-tier banks (SD-AI-I: 36.3%), and the reason is structural rather than a
control failure: literacy-tier items are shorter (mean option length 116 characters vs
166), and a recall item's correct answer is typically a complete, accurate definition
while its distractors are shorter incorrect statements. The control deliberately does
**not** force "key never longest," which would create an equally exploitable inverse
cue; it enforces option-length homogeneity with a small permitted margin.

The metric that determines exploitability is therefore the **margin**, not the binary.
When the AIE-I key is longest, it leads by a mean of **5.1 characters** — a difference
no candidate can perceive — and only **1 item in 128** is longest by a margin large
enough to be visible (≥20 chars), which compares favourably with the SD-AI-I benchmark
bank (1.1%). The length cue is neutralized to a marginal, non-diagnostic lead. Both
figures are published here rather than only the favourable one.

**Honest ceiling.** This audit proves the item bank is **cue-neutral and structurally
sound**. It does **not** establish item difficulty or discrimination, which can only
come from live candidate response data and remain pending (§7). All figures above are
reproducible at any time by re-running the cue-audit queries against the live secure
pool.

**Credential verification.** Every issued credential carries a unique credential code
and a public verification record, allowing any third party to confirm a credential's
authenticity, holder, issue date, and validity status.

## 9. Recertification

AIE-I credentials are valid for **2 years** from issuance.

**Rationale.** The credential certifies literacy in the use of everyday AI tools — a
domain whose tools and norms change quickly, but whose *foundational* competencies
(what a model is, why it hallucinates, why output must be verified, why a human stays
accountable) are more durable than the tooling. A 2-year cycle is set longer than the
1-year cycle used for the practitioner-tier Scrum credentials, reflecting that
durability, while remaining short enough that a current credential is a genuine signal
of current literacy. Recertification re-tests against the then-current body of
knowledge.

## 10. Traceability and coverage

The scheme maintains a complete, queryable traceability matrix:

**domain → task → concept → assessment item**, and
**concept → instructional lesson**

Three audit-relevant guarantees follow, each backed by a live database view or query:

1. **Coverage** — every one of the 16 in-scope tasks is supported by assessment items
   in both pools, in all three languages (verified: every task carries exactly 8
   secure and 10 practice items per language), and is taught by at least one lesson per
   language. Proven live: **47 / 47 concepts taught, 47 / 47 concepts tested, 0
   concepts taught-but-not-practiced.**
2. **No untaught testing** — every concept assessed is present in the published body of
   knowledge, and every examination item resolves back to the lesson(s) that teach its
   concept(s). Proven live: **untaught-testing violations = 0.** This is the ISO/IEC
   17024 "no untaught testing" guarantee, queryable via the coverage view.
3. **Secure firewall** — the secure examination pool carries **zero** links into the
   practice/learning graph, provable by a firewall query returning 0 (§8).

**A note on what the coverage view measures.** The coverage view derives "tested" from
the practice/learning graph (`question_concepts`). Because secure items carry no such
link by design (the firewall), secure-pool sufficiency is proven not by the coverage
view but by the blueprint allocator plus a per-task count (every task at its
8-per-language floor). Two pools, two proofs: practice reach by the coverage view,
secure sufficiency by the allocator and count.

**Construct validity — scenario grounding.** Items must test the intended construct and
nothing else. Because AIE-I certifies non-technical professionals, an item that
required a candidate to parse engineering, product-craft, or agile-framework
vocabulary would test a **second, construct-irrelevant** competency and disadvantage a
capable candidate for reasons unrelated to AI literacy. The item generator is
therefore grounded per credential: AIE-I items are drawn from ordinary workplace
scenarios, and specialist vocabulary from unrelated disciplines is excluded by
construction. Measured on the live English secure pool: **0 items** containing
agile/Scrum framework vocabulary.

The projection of lessons into this matrix, and the generation of assessment items
against the taught concepts, are maintained by automated, re-runnable controls rather
than by hand, so the matrix stays complete as content evolves.

### 10.4 Trilingual equivalence and terminology control

This credential is delivered in English, Latin American Spanish (es-419) and
Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR). The published blueprint in each language states
what the credential attests, so a translated task statement that describes a
different competence than the exam measures would be invalid for every candidate
sitting in that language.

Terminology is governed by **`TERMINOLOGY-POLICY.md`**, the single source of
truth for all Certidemy credentials. It records sixteen rules, each traceable to
a primary source (the official EU AI Act language versions, the official
translated Scrum Guides, the ISO/IEC standards) or to a recorded external-review
decision.

This credential is entry-tier and non-regulated, so no statutory terminology applies. Note one deliberate cross-cert difference: AIE-I renders *human oversight* as `supervision humana`/`supervisao humana` and translates the `human in the loop` gloss into plain language, because the audience is non-technical and the phrase is explanatory rather than a tested distinction. AIGRM-I uses the statutory `vigilancia humana`, and AISM-I keeps `human-in-the-loop` in English at task 4.13 where the in-loop / on-loop distinction is itself examined. All three are correct under the cross-cert rule (policy S3, Rule 2).

**Equivalence is enforced mechanically, not by procedure.** A database trigger
(`trg_invalidate_task_translations`) flips a task's translations back to
provisional in the same transaction whenever its English statement changes, and
the conformance gate (`verify-cert.mjs`, invariant `i18n.approved`) fails any
certification holding a provisional translation. The English cannot move without
the translations declaring themselves stale, and the credential cannot publish
while they are.

## 11. Scheme maintenance and governance

**Review cadence.** Reviewed when the job-task analysis is re-validated, when the
regulatory literacy expectations this credential speaks to change materially, or at
minimum annually.

**Change control.** Changes to domains, weightings, the cognitive blueprint, the item
bank, or the pass mark are versioned and recorded.

**Content completeness.** The examination and practice item banks are complete in all
three languages (384 secure / 480 practice). Instructional lessons are complete in all
three languages (16 lessons × 3 = 48 records). There is no partial-localization caveat
on this credential: study, practice, and examination are all fully available in
English, es-419, and pt-BR.

**Publication status.** This credential is **pre-launch** (`is_published = false`,
status `coming_soon`) at the time of writing: the teaching layer and both item banks
are complete and verified, and publication follows once the launch checklist is
cleared.

**Open items on the path to accreditation** (named transparently):

- Independent SME-panel validation of the job-task analysis and weightings (§5).
- Formal standard-setting study and psychometric validation of the 80% cut score, using
  candidate data collected in live operation (§7).
- Item difficulty and discrimination statistics from live candidate data (§8.1).
- Governance-level impartiality structure separating the certification decision from
  commercial and training functions (§8).
- Operational records required by the standard: appeals, complaints handling,
  examination-integrity / identity procedures, and internal audit.

The credential is positioned as **"built to the ISO/IEC 17024 framework / audit-ready
by design,"** not as accredited, until accreditation is formally achieved.
